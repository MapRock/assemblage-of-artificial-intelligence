A. Problem statement

[OPEN QUESTION] The premise is not settled by the evidence set itself. The factual problem is therefore: **if chickens were present at El Arenal-1 on Chile’s Arauco Peninsula before European contact, what transport mechanism best accounts for their arrival?** The alternative possibility—that the Chilean finds are post-Columbian because of dating or DNA problems—must remain in play until elimination. ([PMC][1])

B. Fact inventory

1. [FACT] El Arenal-1 is on the Arauco Peninsula of Chile. ([PubMed][2])
2. [FACT] The 2007 PNAS paper reported a direct radiocarbon date on an El Arenal-1 chicken bone of **622 ± 35 BP**, calibrated to **AD 1321–1407** at 2σ. ([PNAS][3])
3. [FACT] A 2008 follow-up reported two additional directly dated El Arenal-1 chicken bones: **510 ± 30 BP** calibrated to **AD 1427–1459**, and **506 ± 30 BP** calibrated to **AD 1426–1457** at 2σ. ([PMC][4])
4. [FACT] The same 2008 follow-up reported thermoluminescence dates on associated pottery from El Arenal-1 of **AD 1285–1415** and **AD 1335–1445**. ([PMC][4])
5. [FACT] The 2008 follow-up reported stable-isotope values for two El Arenal-1 chicken bones including **δ13C of -19.85 and -19.45**, **δ15N of 2.6 and 3.5**, and one **δ34S of 2.16**. ([PMC][4])
6. [FACT] Storey et al. stated that these isotope results indicated a terrestrial dietary signature and that no marine calibration was required. ([PMC][4])
7. [FACT] Gongora et al. argued that El Arenal-1 was a midden with marine organisms present and that local isotopic standards were lacking. ([PMC][5])
8. [FACT] Gongora et al. wrote that any marine input into the dated chickens would be consistent with a post-Columbian chronology. ([PMC][5])
9. [FACT] Beavan later wrote that the El Arenal chicken-bone dates were reliable pre-Columbian dates at 2 SD and that there was no evidence for diet offset sufficient to overturn them. ([PMC][6])
10. [FACT] The 2007 paper reported an ancient mtDNA sequence from an El Arenal-1 chicken and linked it to haplogroup **E**. ([PubMed][2])
11. [FACT] Storey and Matisoo-Smith later stated that haplogroup **E** had been identified in ancient chicken remains from both the Pacific and Chile. ([PMC][7])
12. [FACT] Storey and Matisoo-Smith also stated that, among their ancient Pacific chickens, **12 of 15 E haplotypes** came from sites predating **AD 1000**. ([PMC][7])
13. [FACT] Gongora et al. argued that the El Arenal-1 chicken carried a **worldwide genetic signature** potentially compatible with introduction routes via Europe, Asia, or Polynesia. ([PMC][5])
14. [FACT] Gongora et al. also wrote that none of the unusual genetic signatures from Easter Island chickens had been reported from South America. ([PMC][5])
15. [FACT] Thomson et al. in 2014 reported that ancient Polynesian chickens were characterized by a cluster of haplogroup **D** sequences with four diagnostic control-region SNPs, termed the “ancestral Polynesian motif.” ([PMC][8])
16. [FACT] Thomson et al. wrote that no early South American chicken samples had been detected with those diagnostic Polynesian mtDNA haplotypes. ([PMC][8])
17. [FACT] Thomson et al. wrote that previous Pacific chicken studies had been impacted by contamination with modern chicken DNA. ([PMC][8])
18. [FACT] Storey and Matisoo-Smith responded that, in the contamination paper Thomson et al. cited, chicken contamination appeared in **1 of 779** no-template PCR controls. ([PMC][7])
19. [FACT] Storey and Matisoo-Smith wrote that haplogroup **E** was never found in their extraction blanks and that key E sequences from Chile and Vanuatu were reproduced in an independent laboratory with different methods and reagents. ([PMC][7])
20. [FACT] Thomson et al. replied that at least some of the previously reported **E** results were contaminated and that the full extent of contamination could not be determined. ([PMC][9])
21. [FACT] The 2007 PNAS paper states that chicken first appears in Pacific archaeological sites about **3,000 years ago** in the Reef/Santa Cruz area and shortly thereafter in Vanuatu. ([PMC][1])
22. [FACT] Thomson et al. place first colonization of Western Polynesia at roughly **3,250–3,100 cal BP** and describe a later rapid colonization of Remote East Polynesia after a long hiatus. ([PMC][8])
23. [FACT] The 2007 PNAS paper states that the El Arenal chicken dates fall within the expected time range for colonization of eastern Polynesian islands including **Rapa, Pitcairn, and Easter Island**. ([PMC][1])
24. [FACT] Fitzpatrick and Callaghan modeled voyages relevant to chicken transport using departure points including Samoa, Tonga, Niue, the Tuamotus, Hawai‘i, and Easter Island. ([ScienceDirect][10])
25. [FACT] The abstract snippet for that study states that voyage simulations found eastward arrivals to South America could reach **40% probability in favorable seasons** and that return voyages from Chile were generally not possible most times of year. ([ScienceDirect][10])
26. [FACT] Goodwin et al. reported that short eastward passages in the Pacific were possible in seasonal westerlies and that stronger El Niño conditions could create broader trade-wind reversals. ([PMC][11])
27. [FACT] The 2020 Nature study by Ioannidis et al. reported conclusive evidence for prehistoric contact between Polynesian individuals and Native American individuals around **AD 1200**. ([Nature][12])
28. [FACT] The 2014 Current Biology study on Rapanui genomes reported Native American admixture dating to **AD 1280–1495** and European admixture dating to **AD 1850–1895** in the analyzed sample set. ([ScienceDirect][13])
29. [FACT] A 2017 study of ancient Rapanui individuals reported that the analyzed individuals did not show substantial Native American ancestry and that present-day Native American ancestry on Rapa Nui might derive from more recent events. ([PubMed][14])
30. [FACT] A 2024 Nature study on ancient Rapanui genomes reported ancient individuals dated **1670–1950 CE** and estimated Native American admixture at roughly **1250–1430 CE** in ancient and present-day Rapanui. ([Nature][15])
31. [FACT] The 2022 PNAS domestic-chicken synthesis places the first unambiguous domestic chicken bones at Ban Non Wat in central Thailand at about **1650–1250 BCE**. ([PNAS][16])
32. [FACT] The 2021 review on chicken origins states that domestic chickens derive primarily from red junglefowl populations in South and Southeast Asia. ([PMC][17])
33. [FACT] Gongora et al. wrote that European chickens were introduced into the American continents by the Spanish after their arrival in the 15th century. ([PMC][18])
34. [FACT] Storey’s 2011 review states that the first recorded European introduction of a chicken to continental South America was in **AD 1500**, when Pedro Álvares Cabral gifted a chicken in Brazil. ([Evols][19])
35. [FACT] Thomson et al. analyzed **122 modern** and **22 ancient** chicken specimens from Polynesia and Island Southeast Asia in their 2014 study. ([PubMed][20])
36. [FACT] Thomson et al. found connections between chickens in Micronesia and the Bismarck Islands and reported no evidence that these were involved in dispersals further east. ([PMC][21])

C. Factual/clue space

**Entities**

* [RELATION] El Arenal-1 site, Arauco Peninsula, Chile; chicken bones; associated pottery; marine remains in site context are part of the evidentiary set. ([PubMed][2])
* [RELATION] Candidate source regions present in the fact set: South/Southeast Asia as ultimate chicken origin; Polynesia/East Polynesia/Rapa Nui as possible intermediary zones; Europe as a post-1500 source. ([PMC][17])

**Timing constraints**

* [CONSTRAINT] If the El Arenal chicken radiocarbon dates are valid without major reservoir distortion, the chickens were present in Chile between the 14th and mid-15th centuries. ([PNAS][3])
* [CONSTRAINT] That window overlaps the period after East Polynesian expansion and near the settlement horizon of Rapa Nui/East Polynesia referenced in the 2007 paper. ([PMC][1])
* [CONSTRAINT] A European source requires either rejection or substantial weakening of those radiocarbon dates, because the first recorded European chicken introduction to continental South America in this fact set is AD 1500. ([Evols][19])
* [OPEN QUESTION] Whether diet offset or site context could shift the El Arenal dates enough to make them post-1500 remains disputed within the source set. ([PMC][5])

**Transport mechanisms**

* [RELATION] Chickens are a human-transported domestic species whose ultimate origin lies in Asia, not the Americas. ([PMC][17])
* [CONSTRAINT] Any pre-Columbian arrival in Chile requires a transoceanic human-mediated mechanism somewhere in the chain. ([PMC][17])
* [RELATION] Voyage modeling literature in the source set includes eastward Polynesia-to-South-America scenarios and reports seasonal windows favorable to such crossings. ([ScienceDirect][10])
* [CONSTRAINT] Return voyages from Chile are modeled as difficult for much of the year in the voyage-simulation snippet. ([ScienceDirect][10])
* [INFERENCE] If chickens moved from Polynesia to Chile pre-Columbian, the movement need not have been a large colonizing migration; it could have been a one-off canoe landfall, a storm-displaced voyage, or contact followed by local coastal exchange. Supported by the existence of feasible eastward passages and the difficulty of routine returns. ([ScienceDirect][10])

**Biological bottlenecks**

* [CONSTRAINT] Transport requires live birds, eggs, or very recent movement of breeding stock; bones alone do not move populations.
* [CONSTRAINT] A lasting archaeological signature at El Arenal implies at least temporary survival after arrival.
* [INFERENCE] Live chicken carriage is mechanically easier in canoes or ships than transporting many large mammals; that keeps one-off human carriage viable.

**DNA / haplogroup issues**

* [RELATION] El Arenal’s reported ancient mtDNA falls in haplogroup E. ([PubMed][2])
* [CONSTRAINT] Haplogroup E is not route-diagnostic within this source set because one side treats it as ancient Pacific and Chilean, while the other side treats it as globally widespread. ([PMC][7])
* [CONSTRAINT] Thomson’s Pacific-diagnostic signal is haplogroup D with a four-SNP “ancestral Polynesian motif,” and no early South American sample in that study carried it. ([PMC][8])
* [OPEN QUESTION] Whether the El Arenal E sequence is authentic ancient DNA or partly explained by contamination remains unresolved inside the evidence set. ([PMC][8])

**Linguistic / cultural implications**

* [RELATION] This evidence set contains no direct linguistic record tying El Arenal chickens to a specific source population.
* [INFERENCE] Any hypothesis requiring broad, durable cultural transfer should ideally leave more than isolated faunal evidence; a single or narrow contact event requires less.

**Dating / contamination issues**

* [OPEN QUESTION] Are the chicken-bone dates secure against marine reservoir effects? The source set contains direct disagreement. ([PMC][5])
* [OPEN QUESTION] Are the El Arenal mtDNA sequences secure against low-level contamination? The source set contains direct disagreement. ([PMC][8])
* [CONSTRAINT] The radiocarbon argument and the DNA argument are partially separable: even if route-diagnostic DNA is weak, pre-1500 dates still demand a non-European mechanism unless the dates fail. ([PMC][4])

D. Candidate hypotheses

1. [HYPOTHESIS] **Direct Polynesian transport to southern Chile.** Polynesian voyagers intentionally carried live chickens eastward and landed on the South American coast, with El Arenal reflecting that introduction.
2. [HYPOTHESIS] **Polynesian contact with another part of South America, followed by indigenous coastal transfer to Arauco.** The trans-Pacific step is Polynesian; the final step to El Arenal is South American exchange.
3. [HYPOTHESIS] **Accidental Polynesian transport.** A storm-driven or otherwise unintended eastward voyage from Polynesia reached South America with live chickens aboard.
4. [HYPOTHESIS] **Pre-Columbian non-Polynesian Old World transport.** Some other Asian or transoceanic mariner brought chickens to South America before Columbus.
5. [HYPOTHESIS] **Post-Columbian European introduction misdated as pre-Columbian.** The El Arenal chickens reached Chile only after European contact, and the pre-1500 signal is an artifact of reservoir effects, sample issues, contamination, or interpretive error.

E. Deductions from each hypothesis

**1. Direct Polynesian transport to southern Chile**

* [DEDUCTION] Timing predicted: after East Polynesian expansion and before European arrival; the El Arenal 14th–15th century dates fit this window. ([PMC][4])
* [DEDUCTION] Route predicted: East Polynesia or Rapa Nui to the Chilean coast through seasonal westerlies or favorable climate windows. ([ScienceDirect][10])
* [DEDUCTION] Biological form transported: live chickens or fertile eggs.
* [DEDUCTION] Linguistic consequence predicted: possible but not necessary lexical or cultural traces of Polynesian contact on the South American coast.
* [DEDUCTION] Archaeological consequence predicted: pre-1500 chicken remains on the Pacific coast, possibly concentrated in coastal nodes rather than deep inland.
* [DEDUCTION] DNA consequence predicted: an early Pacific-compatible lineage should be present, but it need not be uniquely route-diagnostic if lineage turnover or mixed source stocks existed. ([PMC][7])
* [DEDUCTION] Contamination/dating vulnerability: weakened if the E sequence is non-diagnostic or contaminated; survives if the radiocarbon dates stand independently. ([PMC][8])
* [DEDUCTION] Contact intensity implied: one-time or repeated contact both remain possible.

**2. Polynesian contact elsewhere on South America, then indigenous coastal transfer**

* [DEDUCTION] Timing predicted: same pre-1500 outer window, but chickens could reach El Arenal somewhat later than first landfall elsewhere on the continent.
* [DEDUCTION] Route predicted: Polynesian landfall on another Pacific-coast region of South America, then overland/coastal exchange southward to Arauco.
* [DEDUCTION] Biological form transported: initially live chickens or eggs across the Pacific; later live birds passed through indigenous networks.
* [DEDUCTION] Linguistic consequence predicted: weaker Polynesian linguistic signal at El Arenal than at first-contact points.
* [DEDUCTION] Archaeological consequence predicted: earlier or parallel pre-1500 chicken finds north of Arauco would be compatible.
* [DEDUCTION] DNA consequence predicted: Pacific-origin lineages might appear diluted, mixed, or route-ambiguous by the time they reached Chile.
* [DEDUCTION] Contamination/dating vulnerability: same as Hypothesis 1 for the El Arenal specimens, but this model is less dependent on El Arenal being the first South American landing point.
* [DEDUCTION] Contact intensity implied: at least one trans-Pacific contact plus regional indigenous transmission.

**3. Accidental Polynesian transport**

* [DEDUCTION] Timing predicted: same general East Polynesian/pre-1500 window.
* [DEDUCTION] Route predicted: storm displacement or drift from East Polynesia/Rapa Nui toward South America, consistent with eastward passage windows. ([ScienceDirect][10])
* [DEDUCTION] Biological form transported: a small number of live birds aboard a canoe or voyaging craft.
* [DEDUCTION] Linguistic consequence predicted: very slight or absent linguistic signature.
* [DEDUCTION] Archaeological consequence predicted: sparse, localized evidence rather than broad cultural transfer.
* [DEDUCTION] DNA consequence predicted: narrow founder signal, perhaps a single lineage.
* [DEDUCTION] Contamination/dating vulnerability: same El Arenal dating vulnerabilities remain; this hypothesis depends less on a clear Polynesian-specific haplotype than deliberate-settlement models do.
* [DEDUCTION] Contact intensity implied: one-off or very low-frequency events.

**4. Pre-Columbian non-Polynesian Old World transport**

* [DEDUCTION] Timing predicted: any pre-1500 date is allowable.
* [DEDUCTION] Route predicted: direct or indirect trans-Pacific arrival from Asia or another Old World maritime network.
* [DEDUCTION] Biological form transported: live birds or eggs.
* [DEDUCTION] Linguistic consequence predicted: no Polynesian signal; perhaps none at all if contact was transient.
* [DEDUCTION] Archaeological consequence predicted: additional signs of non-Polynesian Old World contact should be possible somewhere in South America, not only a few chicken bones.
* [DEDUCTION] DNA consequence predicted: route-ambiguous Old World haplotypes such as widespread E are compatible. ([PMC][5])
* [DEDUCTION] Contamination/dating vulnerability: less sensitive to the loss of Polynesian-specific DNA, but still depends on the dates being genuinely pre-1500.
* [DEDUCTION] Contact intensity implied: at least one otherwise poorly attested transoceanic episode.

**5. Post-Columbian European introduction misdated as pre-Columbian**

* [DEDUCTION] Timing predicted: after AD 1500, despite older apparent radiocarbon ages. ([Evols][19])
* [DEDUCTION] Route predicted: European ships to the Americas, then colonial diffusion to Chile.
* [DEDUCTION] Biological form transported: ordinary colonial chickens.
* [DEDUCTION] Linguistic consequence predicted: no Polynesian signal required.
* [DEDUCTION] Archaeological consequence predicted: no secure pre-1500 chicken context should survive reanalysis.
* [DEDUCTION] DNA consequence predicted: worldwide non-diagnostic lineages such as E are compatible. ([PMC][5])
* [DEDUCTION] Contamination/dating vulnerability: this hypothesis gains strength only if marine reservoir effects, context mixing, or aDNA contamination materially undermine the El Arenal evidence. ([PMC][5])
* [DEDUCTION] Contact intensity implied: ordinary repeated colonial transport.

F. Elimination/pruning

**Hypothesis 1: Direct Polynesian transport to southern Chile**
[SURVIVES]
Reason: It fits the direct radiocarbon dates if those dates stand; it fits the known chronology of East Polynesian expansion; and voyage models in the source set provide a physically workable eastward route. Its main weakness is not timing or route feasibility but DNA specificity: El Arenal’s reported E haplotype is disputed as either ancient Pacific-compatible or too widespread to prove a Polynesian source, and Thomson’s Pacific-diagnostic D motif was not found in early South American samples. ([PMC][4])

**Hypothesis 2: Polynesian contact elsewhere on South America, then indigenous coastal transfer**
[SURVIVES]
Reason: It matches the pre-1500 dates while requiring less than a direct landfall specifically at Arauco. It also tolerates route-ambiguous DNA better than Hypothesis 1, because lineage mixing or secondary movement inside South America could blur a clean Polynesian signature by the time birds reached Chile. Nothing in the fact set contradicts it directly. ([PMC][4])

**Hypothesis 3: Accidental Polynesian transport**
[WEAKENED]
Reason: Eastward passages are physically feasible, so it is not contradicted. But it adds an extra contingency—unintended landfall with surviving live chickens—without explaining the South American-side persistence any better than deliberate contact does. It remains possible because the evidence set does not require large-scale cultural transfer. ([ScienceDirect][10])

**Hypothesis 4: Pre-Columbian non-Polynesian Old World transport**
[WEAKENED]
Reason: It can absorb the non-diagnostic E haplotype and pre-1500 dates, but it requires an otherwise poorly evidenced transoceanic mechanism not independently supported by the present fact set. By contrast, the fact set does contain voyage-feasibility studies for Polynesian routes and genetic evidence for prehistoric Polynesian–Native American contact. ([PMC][5])

**Hypothesis 5: Post-Columbian European introduction misdated as pre-Columbian**
[WEAKENED]
Reason: This hypothesis directly conflicts with three El Arenal direct dates that calibrate before 1500 and with associated pottery dates in the same broad range. It survives only because the source set contains unresolved objections about marine input, site context, and DNA contamination. It is not eliminated because the factual dispute is real, but it bears the burden of overturning multiple pre-1500 dates rather than explaining a single ambiguous clue. ([PNAS][3])

G. Best surviving explanation

The best surviving explanation is:

**A human-mediated Polynesian introduction, probably from East Polynesia, with chickens carried alive across the Pacific before European contact, followed either by direct landing on the Chilean coast or by first contact elsewhere on the Pacific coast of South America and then indigenous transfer to Arauco.** ([PMC][4])

Why this survives best after elimination:

* It explains the pre-1500 radiocarbon dates without having to break them. ([PNAS][3])
* It uses a route family for which this evidence set contains explicit voyage-feasibility support. ([ScienceDirect][10])
* It is reinforced, at the level of contact feasibility, by later genomic evidence for prehistoric Polynesian–Native American contact. ([Nature][12])
* It does not require the El Arenal mtDNA to be uniquely Polynesian; the dates do most of the work, while the DNA only needs to remain compatible with a Polynesian carriage scenario. ([PMC][7])

Within that surviving family, I would keep **two sub-versions alive**:

1. direct Polynesian landfall in southern Chile;
2. Polynesian contact elsewhere on the Pacific coast, then South American redistribution to Arauco.

The current fact set does not force a clean choice between those two.

H. Residual uncertainties

The main unresolved points are these:

* Whether the El Arenal radiocarbon dates are fully secure against marine/dietary offset. The evidence set contains explicit disagreement. ([PMC][5])
* Whether the reported El Arenal haplogroup E sequence is authentic ancient DNA or partly a contamination artifact. The evidence set also contains explicit disagreement here. ([PMC][8])
* Whether Arauco was the first South American landing zone or only a later receiving point inside a wider exchange network.
* Whether the contact was deliberate, accidental, singular, or repeated.
* Whether future directly dated pre-1500 chicken finds elsewhere on the Pacific coast would point to a broader coastal diffusion pattern.

I. Comparison with prevailing theory

Outside this exercise, I would normally answer more cautiously: **the claim of pre-Columbian chickens in Chile remains disputed, because the radiocarbon dates have been challenged and the El Arenal mtDNA is not universally accepted as route-diagnostic Polynesian evidence.** ([PMC][8])

Inside the reasoning structure you asked for, once the evidence is organized and the hypotheses are pruned, the best surviving mechanism is still **Polynesian human transport across the Pacific**, not a natural crossing and not an ordinary colonial introduction. The difference is that I am keeping the premise-sensitive issues open until elimination, rather than starting from the mainstream caution by default.
