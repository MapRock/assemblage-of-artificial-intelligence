# Pre-Columbian Chickens in South America: Holmes-Style Reasoning

**A. Restated Problem**  
How did chickens arrive in pre-Columbian South America, such as the Arauco Peninsula in Chile?

**B. Fact Inventory**

1. Chicken bones were recovered from the El Arenal-1 archaeological site on the Arauco Peninsula in Chile. **[FACT]**  
2. One chicken bone from El Arenal-1 was radiocarbon dated to 1304–1424 AD. **[FACT]**  
3. Mitochondrial DNA sequences from chicken bones at El Arenal-1 match haplotypes found in ancient Polynesian chicken bones. **[FACT]**  
4. Chickens were domesticated from red junglefowl in Southeast Asia. **[FACT]**  
5. The oldest chicken bones from archaeological sites are from Ban Non Wat in central Thailand, dated to 3250–3650 years ago. **[FACT]**  
6. Chicken bones appear in archaeological sites in Vanuatu and Tonga dated to 3000–2800 years before present. **[FACT]**  
7. Polynesians settled Easter Island around 1200 AD. **[FACT]**  
8. The distance from Easter Island to the coast of South America is approximately 3700 kilometers. **[FACT]**  
9. Genome-wide analysis of modern individuals from Polynesian islands and Pacific coast South American populations shows genetic admixture dated to around 1150–1230 AD. **[FACT]**  
10. Sweet potatoes are native to South America. **[FACT]**  
11. Sweet potato remains from archaeological sites in the Cook Islands are dated to around 1000 AD. **[FACT]**  
12. The Polynesian word for sweet potato is "kumara". **[FACT]**  
13. There are no native chicken species in the Americas. **[FACT]**  
14. Christopher Columbus brought chickens to the Americas in 1493 AD. **[FACT]**  
15. The South Pacific gyre ocean current flows counterclockwise, from the eastern Pacific toward the western Pacific. **[FACT]**  
16. Polynesian voyagers used outrigger canoes. **[FACT]**  
17. Polynesian navigation methods included observation of stars, ocean swells, winds, waves, and birds. **[FACT]**  
18. Indigenous peoples in South America used non-Spanish names for chickens in the 16th century. **[FACT]**  
19. The Araucana chicken breed in Chile lays blue eggs. **[FACT]**  
20. Chicken bones from archaeological sites in Haiti and Florida date to the 1500s and 1600s AD. **[FACT]**

**C. Constraint / Relationship Space**

- **Entities**  
  Chickens (domesticated birds requiring human care for long-distance transport)  
  Red junglefowl (wild ancestor native to Southeast Asia)  
  Polynesians (human group with seafaring technology)  
  South Americans (human groups native to the continent)  
  Sweet potatoes (plant native to South America)  
  Ocean currents (environmental flows affecting drift)  
  Winds (environmental forces affecting sailing)  
  Outrigger canoes (vessels used for ocean voyages)

  - **[RELATION]** Chickens are connected to Polynesians through archaeological bones in Polynesian sites.  
  - **[RELATION]** Sweet potatoes are connected to Polynesians through archaeological remains in Polynesian sites.  
  - **[RELATION]** Polynesians are connected to South Americans through genetic admixture in modern descendants.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** No wild chicken ancestors exist in the Americas.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Chickens require live transport or viable eggs for translocation across oceans.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Ocean voyages must occur before 1492 AD for pre-Columbian arrival.  
  - **[OPEN QUESTION]** Specific route taken between Polynesia and South America.

- **Transport mechanisms**  
  Human voyaging (deliberate sailing in canoes)  
  Drift (passive movement via currents or winds)  
  Accidental human-assisted drift (e.g., boats carried off course)

  - **[RELATION]** Human voyaging connects to navigation methods using stars, swells, winds, waves, and birds.  
  - **[RELATION]** Drift connects to ocean currents flowing from east to west in the South Pacific.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Chickens cannot survive unassisted ocean drift without food, water, or protection.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Eggs have limited viability period for hatching after transport.  
  - **[OPEN QUESTION]** Whether transport involved live birds, eggs, or both.

- **Biological bottlenecks**  
  Chicken survival on voyages (need for sustenance and shelter)  
  Breeding viability post-arrival (minimum population for reproduction)  
  No native American species for hybridization

  - **[RELATION]** Chicken domestication connects to Southeast Asia origin.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Chickens must be transported live or as fertile eggs to establish populations.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Araucana breed traits (blue eggs) appear in some Asian chicken varieties.  
  - **[OPEN QUESTION]** Genetic stability of transported chickens over generations.

- **Timing constraints**  
  Domestication after 3650 years ago  
  Polynesian settlement of Easter Island by 1200 AD  
  El Arenal-1 bones dated to 1304–1424 AD  
  Genetic admixture around 1150–1230 AD  
  Sweet potato in Polynesia by 1000 AD

  - **[RELATION]** El Arenal-1 date overlaps with Polynesian settlement periods.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** All events must predate 1492 AD.  
  - **[OPEN QUESTION]** Exact sequence of multiple potential contacts.

- **Linguistic or cultural implications**  
  Non-Spanish indigenous names for chickens  
  "kumara" similarity to South American terms for sweet potato

  - **[RELATION]** Linguistic terms connect to cultural exchange.  
  - **[CONSTRAINT]** Names indicate pre-European knowledge.  
  - **[OPEN QUESTION]** Direction of linguistic borrowing.

**D. Candidate Hypotheses**

1. Polynesian voyagers sailed to South America and introduced chickens during contact. **[HYPOTHESIS]**  
2. South American voyagers sailed to Polynesia, acquired chickens there, and returned with them. **[HYPOTHESIS]**  
3. Chickens arrived via accidental drift from Polynesia, assisted by humans on off-course vessels. **[HYPOTHESIS]**  
4. Early non-Polynesian Asian voyagers directly introduced chickens to South America. **[HYPOTHESIS]**  
5. The chicken bones in pre-Columbian South America are from post-Columbian intrusions or misdated contexts. **[HYPOTHESIS]**

**E. Deductions from Each Hypothesis**

**Hypothesis 1**  
- Timing predicts introduction around 1150–1424 AD. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Route predicts from eastern Polynesia (e.g., Easter Island) to South American coast against prevailing currents. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Biological form transported is live domesticated chickens or eggs. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Linguistic consequences predict Polynesian influence on South American chicken names. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Archaeological consequences predict Polynesian artifacts or chicken bones in South American sites dated to contact period. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Implies possible repeated contact for genetic admixture and plant exchange. **[DEDUCTION]**

**Hypothesis 2**  
- Timing predicts introduction around 1150–1424 AD. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Route predicts from South American coast to eastern Polynesia with currents, then return against currents. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Biological form transported is live domesticated chickens or eggs acquired in Polynesia. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Linguistic consequences predict South American influence on Polynesian chicken names. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Archaeological consequences predict South American artifacts or sweet potato remains in Polynesian sites dated to contact period. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Implies possible one-time contact if focused on return voyage. **[DEDUCTION]**

**Hypothesis 3**  
- Timing predicts introduction around 1150–1424 AD or earlier. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Route predicts passive drift following ocean currents from Polynesia to South America. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Biological form transported is live chickens or eggs on drifting vessels. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Linguistic consequences predict limited or no new terms if accidental. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Archaeological consequences predict isolated chicken bones without cultural artifacts. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Implies one-time events. **[DEDUCTION]**

**Hypothesis 4**  
- Timing predicts introduction after domestication but before 1492 AD. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Route predicts direct from Southeast Asia across Pacific to South America. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Biological form transported is early domesticated chickens. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Linguistic consequences predict Asian influence on South American chicken names. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Archaeological consequences predict Asian artifacts in South American sites. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Implies repeated contact for establishment. **[DEDUCTION]**

**Hypothesis 5**  
- Timing predicts post-1492 AD actual arrival. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Route predicts none pre-Columbian. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Biological form transported is irrelevant pre-Columbian. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Linguistic consequences predict Spanish-origin names only. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Archaeological consequences predict no pre-Columbian chicken bones. **[DEDUCTION]**  
- Implies no pre-Columbian contact. **[DEDUCTION]**

**F. Elimination / Pruning**

- **Hypothesis 1**: Survives, as it aligns with genetic admixture dates, sweet potato presence in Polynesia, El Arenal-1 dates and DNA matches, and Polynesian navigation capabilities. **[SURVIVES]**  
- **Hypothesis 2**: Weakened by ocean currents favoring travel from South America to Polynesia but not easy return, and lack of evidence for South American seafaring technology matching Polynesian capabilities. **[WEAKENED]**  
- **Hypothesis 3**: Eliminated by chicken survival constraints on long unassisted drifts and currents flowing opposite direction (westward). **[ELIMINATED]**  
- **Hypothesis 4**: Eliminated by no archaeological evidence of direct Asian artifacts in pre-Columbian South America and timing constraints post-domestication not matching direct route without intermediate stops. **[ELIMINATED]**  
- **Hypothesis 5**: Weakened by radiocarbon dates on El Arenal-1 bones predating 1492 AD and non-Spanish indigenous names for chickens. **[WEAKENED]**

**G. Best Surviving Explanation**  
Polynesian voyagers sailed to South America and introduced chickens during contact around 1150–1424 AD, involving possible repeated interactions that also facilitated genetic admixture and sweet potato exchange.

**H. Residual Uncertainties**  
The exact route and number of voyages remain unresolved due to lack of direct archaeological evidence of Polynesian vessels or settlements in South America. Potential contamination in ancient chicken DNA analyses creates uncertainty in haplotype matches. Direction of primary travel (Polynesia to South America or vice versa) is unclear, as currents favor one way but navigation skills could enable both.
