### Generalized Approach to Fact-Checking

Fact-checking involves a systematic, evidence-based process to verify claims, minimizing bias and ensuring reliability. A key starting point is categorizing the claim based on its nature, as different categories require tailored procedures. This categorization helps determine the most relevant sources, methods, and potential pitfalls (e.g., misinformation in health vs. historical domains).
Below, I'll outline common categories of claims for fact-checking, derived from typical internet or media assertions. These are not exhaustive but cover broad types. For each category, I'll describe:

Key characteristics: What defines claims in this group.
General procedure: High-level steps to verify.
Chain of thought questions: Generalized questions to guide reasoning, structured as a sequence (e.g., initial assessment, sourcing, analysis, conclusion). These can be adapted into a table format for tracking, as in your example (with columns for: Step Description, Answer/Findings, Start/End Timestamp).

This approach emphasizes cross-verification from multiple reputable sources, awareness of context (e.g., outdated data), and logical evaluation.
